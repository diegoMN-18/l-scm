Supply Chain Management - Learning Outcomes Analysis

Based on the lecture slides provided, this document extracts and summarizes the key information related to each learning outcome.

Learning Outcome 1: Recognise the potential role that supply chains can play in the success of private/public/nonprofit organisations

Key Concepts:

SCM as Key Ingredient for Success

  • Supply Chain Management (SCM) and logistics are key ingredients for success in today's competitive global environment
  • The first decade of the 21st century was a period of rapid change for most organisations
  • External forces require firms to be more responsive to survive in intensely competitive global markets

Competitive Advantage through SCM

  • Supply chains contribute to competitive advantage by improving both efficiency (cost reduction) and effectiveness (customer service improvement)
  • Effective SCM creates measurable performance advantages
  • Best-in-class companies have significantly lower supply chain costs (7% vs 13.1% for median companies)

Value Creation

  • Porter's Value Chain explains that logistics is a primary value-adding activity
  • Supply chain activities are key because they influence both cost and service performance
  • Logistics becomes a VALUE ADDING activity, not just a support function

Impact of Effective SCM Effective supply chain management leads to:

  • Lower operating costs
  • Reduced inventory levels (e.g., reducing from 104 days to 61 days can save $30 billion annually)
  • Faster order fulfillment
  • Improved customer service
  • Better working capital utilization

Role Across Organisation Types

  • The benefits apply to private, public, and nonprofit organisations
  • SCM helps organisations adapt to survive in competitive markets
  • Integrated supply chains create competitive advantage

Learning Outcome 2: Discuss the most prominent forces that are driving the rate of change in today's world

Six Major External Forces Driving Change:

1. Globalization

Definition: The concept of a global market-place or global economy

Key Characteristics:

  • Time and distance have been compressed (more connected)
  • Replaced the post-World War II Cold War as the dominant driving force in world economics
  • Most frequently cited factor of change by business leaders

Key Strategic Questions for Global Networks:

  1. Where to source?
  2. Where to manufacture?
  3. Where to store and distribute?
  4. Where (and how) to market & sell?

Challenges of Globalization:

  • Creates more economic and political risk
  • Shorter product life cycles
  • Blurring of traditional organizational boundaries

Business Environment Volatility:

  • Acts of terrorism (ISIS, pirate attacks on ships)
  • Climate change and pandemics (Hurricanes, floods, Covid-19)

Inventory Management Challenges:

  • Faster duplication of products & services
  • Faster reduction in demand
  • Requirement of new pricing policies
  • Higher risk of obsolescence (outdated/expired)

Longer and More Complex Supply Chain Challenges:

  • Growth and increased scope of outsourcing
  • Maintaining financial visibility and value adding services

2. Technology

Role: Technology is a facilitator of internal processes and supply chain transformation. It is also a major force in changing the dynamics of the marketplace.

Key Technological Drivers:

  • The Internet: "Connected" 24/7
  • Social Networks: Impact on customer demand and the speed of information transfers
  • World "Knowledge Pool": Connection through apps and interfaces creates opportunities for collaboration in supply chains

3. Organization Consolidation and Power Shifts

Historical Shift (WW2 → 1980s/90s):

  • Economic power and the driving force in SCs shifted from product manufacturers to the retail end of the supply chain
  • More collaboration among organizations in supply chains

Collaboration Examples:

  • Win-win, improved services such as:
    • Scheduled deliveries
    • "Rainbow" pallets
    • Advance shipments notices (ASNs)
    • Shrink-wrapped pallets
  • Sharing of point-of-sale data to mitigate "bullwhip effect" (information sharing)
  • This allows for more efficient + effective operations & economies of scale

4. Empowered Consumers

Key Characteristic: Knowledge is power

Consumer Empowerment:

  • Consumers have expanded access to product sources and related information
  • Increased buying power due to high income levels

Increased Pressures on Supply Chains:

  • Competitive prices
  • High quality in products and services
  • Tailored or customized products
  • Demographics changes (households now vs 50 years ago)
  • Convenience and responsiveness – 24/7 availability with minimum wait time
  • Flexibility – Omnichannel distribution strategies

5. Government Policy and Regulation

Impact: Government impacts business and their supply chains through policies, regulations, taxes, etc.

Deregulation:

  • Important sectors deregulated since 1970s: transportation, communication, financial institutions
  • Up to 1990 South Africa's economic landscape was heavily regulated, even more so than the USA
  • Examples of regulated areas:
    • Transport regulation
    • KWV and Capespan
    • Agricultural boards

6. Sustainability

The pursuit of sustainability is widely recognized as a key element of successful supply chain management. This is critical to effective risk management and achieving competitive advantage.

Three Pillars of Sustainability:

Society:

  • Focus on people is a significant concern in the area of sustainability
  • There are many ways in which supply chains may help to achieve desired outcomes

Environment:

  • The objective of being "green" is a key element of making positive contributions to improving our environment
  • Supply chains can contribute to environmental outcomes

Economy:

  • Continued economic and financial sustainability is essential to making future positive impacts on society and the environment

Learning Outcome 3: Discuss the development of "supply chain management"

Evolution of Supply Chain Management Concept

Historical Development Stages:

Stage 1: Physical Distribution (1960s)

Focus:

  • Outbound logistics
  • Transportation
  • Warehousing
  • Distribution of finished goods

Rationale:

  • Initial focus on physical distribution or outbound logistics was logical since finished goods (higher value)
  • System relationship between transportation, inventory, warehousing, exterior packaging, materials handling was recognised

Stage 2: Integrated Logistics (1980s)

Focus:

  • Inbound logistics
  • Outbound logistics
  • Inventory management
  • Materials handling

Rationale:

  • The underlying logic that systems and total cost concept was the rationale for logistics management

Stage 3: Supply Chain Management (1990s onwards)

Focus:

  • Integration expanded beyond the firm
  • Suppliers
  • Manufacturers
  • Distributors
  • Retailers

Goal:

  • End-to-end coordination of flows across organisations

Why Does Supply Chain Management Exist?

Historical Problem:

  • Historically, organisations optimised departments individually:
    • Transportation minimised transport costs
    • Warehousing minimised storage costs
    • Procurement minimised purchasing prices

Result of Functional Silos:

  • Higher total organisational cost
  • Poor coordination between activities

Solution:

  • Supply Chain Management integrates activities to optimize the entire system

Functional Silo Concept

What is a Functional Silo?

  • A situation where departments operate independently with limited coordination
  • Each function focuses on its own goals rather than overall organisational performance
  • Information and decisions are not effectively shared across departments

Why Avoid Functional Silos?

  • Leads to higher total supply chain costs
  • Causes poor coordination and slower decision-making
  • Reduces responsiveness to customer demand
  • Prevents system-wide optimization and efficiency

The Total Cost Concept

Key Principle:

  • Decisions should minimise the total supply chain cost, not the cost of individual activities

Cost Relationships:

  • Transport cost
  • Inventory cost
  • Warehousing cost
  • Service cost

These costs are interrelated and must be considered together.

Definition of Supply Chain

A supply chain is the extended network of organisations, activities and logistics systems involved in the flow of materials, services and related information from the supplier's supplier to the customer's customer.

Industry Evidence Supporting SCM

Study 1 – Grocery Manufacturers Association (GMA):

  • Commissioned to analyse the supply chains of grocery manufacturers
  • Indicated potential savings of $30 billion a year by reducing inventory levels from 104 days to 61 days

Study 2 – Supply Chain Council (SCC):

  • Comparison for 1996 and 1997 of "best-in-class" companies (top 10%) and median companies
  • Finding: In 1996, BIC companies spent 7% on SC-costs, compared to 13.1% for median companies

Porter's Value Chain and Supply Chains

Key Points:

  • Porter's Value Chain explains how different activities inside a firm work together to create value for customers
  • Logistics and supply chain activities are key because they influence both cost and service performance
  • Logistics is a primary value-adding activity
  • Inbound and outbound logistics are primary components of a value chain

Development Timeline Summary

Activity Fragmentation (to 1960s):

  • Demand forecasting
  • Inbound
  • Purchasing
  • Requirements planning
  • Production planning
  • Manufacturing inventory
  • Warehousing
  • Material handling
  • Packaging
  • Finished goods inventory
  • Distribution planning
  • Outbound
  • Order processing
  • Transportation
  • Customer services
  • Strategic planning
  • Information services
  • Marketing/Sales
  • Finance

Activity Integration (1980s):

  • Purchasing/Materials Management
  • Integrated Logistics
  • Physical Distribution

1990s+:

  • Supply Chain Management

Learning Outcome 4: Recognise the major supply chain challenges and issues faced by organisations currently and in the future

11 Major Supply Chain Issues

The Big Picture: SCM improves efficiency + effectiveness... but only if these issues are managed.

Issue 1: Supply Chain Networks (Facilities + Flows)

What Can Go Wrong:

  • Slow or expensive supply chain footprint

Key Challenges:

  • Designing optimal network of facilities
  • Managing flows between facilities
  • Balancing cost and service across the network

Issue 2: Complexity

What Can Go Wrong:

  • Errors, inefficiency, and coordination difficulties

Why Supply Chains Get Messy Fast:

  • Global operations across multiple countries
  • Many SKUs (Stock Keeping Units) to manage
  • Multiple suppliers and partners
  • Interconnected dependencies

Issue 3: Inventory Deployment (Where Do We Hold Stock?)

What Can Go Wrong:

  • High inventory costs and bullwhip effect

Key Decisions:

  • Where to position inventory in the network
  • How much to hold at each location
  • Balancing service levels with carrying costs

Issue 4: Information (Data Doesn't = Decisions)

What Can Go Wrong:

  • Poor decision-making due to inaccurate or delayed data

Key Challenges:

  • Ensuring data quality
  • Sharing information across the supply chain
  • Converting data into actionable insights
  • Real-time visibility

Issue 5: Cost vs Value (Efficiency vs Effectiveness)

What Can Go Wrong:

  • Incorrect trade-offs leading to higher total system cost

Key Trade-offs:

  • Balancing cost reduction with service improvement
  • Short-term savings vs long-term value
  • Individual activity optimization vs total cost optimization

Issue 6: Organisational Relationships (Internal + External)

What Can Go Wrong:

  • Functional silos, poor collaboration, and conflict

Key Challenges:

  • Internal coordination across departments
  • External collaboration with suppliers and customers
  • Building trust and sharing information
  • Managing conflicts of interest

Issue 7: Performance Measurement (Link Levels Properly)

What Can Go Wrong:

  • Wrong behaviour due to poorly designed metrics

Key Challenges:

  • Designing metrics that align with organisational goals
  • Measuring across multiple levels of the supply chain
  • Avoiding unintended consequences
  • Linking operational metrics to financial performance

Issue 8: Technology (Don't Throw Tech at the Problem)

What Can Go Wrong:

  • Expensive system failures or poor implementation

Key Considerations:

  • Technology should support business processes, not drive them
  • Proper planning and change management
  • Integration with existing systems
  • User adoption and training
  • Process-first, technology-second approach

Issue 9: Transportation (The Glue)

What Can Go Wrong:

  • Delivery delays and service breakdowns

Key Challenges:

  • Managing transportation across multiple modes and carriers
  • Balancing cost with service levels
  • Dealing with capacity constraints
  • Regulatory compliance

Issue 10: Supply Chain Security (Disruptions Are Normal Now)

What Can Go Wrong:

  • Disruptions, theft, and operational losses

Key Challenges:

  • Managing risk from terrorism, piracy, and cyber threats
  • Dealing with climate change and pandemics
  • Supply chain continuity planning
  • Building resilience

Issue 11: Talent Management (People Are the Capability)

What Can Go Wrong:

  • Weak execution due to skills shortages

Key Challenges:

  • Recruiting skilled supply chain professionals
  • Training and development
  • Retaining talent
  • Building supply chain capabilities

The Bullwhip Effect

Definition: Small changes in customer demand can create large demand fluctuations upstream in the supply chain.

Results:

  • Excess inventory
  • Stockouts
  • Higher operating costs
  • Reduced service performance

Key Characteristic: The further upstream the supply chain, the greater the demand distortion.

Causes of the Bullwhip Effect:

  1. Forecasting errors
  2. Lack of information sharing
  3. Order batching
  4. Promotions
  5. Long lead times

Solution: Improved SC visibility reduces the bullwhip effect.

Supply Chain Flows

Supply chains operate through multiple interconnected flows that move across organisations.

4 Major Supply Chain Flows:

  1. Product/Services Flow:
    • Physical movement of goods and materials
    • Traditionally the "life blood" of the supply chain
    • Customers expect orders in a timely, reliable and damage-free manner
    • Two-way flow (forward and reverse logistics)
  2. Information Flow:
    • Enabling physical flow of products
    • Decision making
    • Supply chain collaborations
    • Can be seen as the "fuel" of a SC
    • Traditionally viewed as flowing opposite direction of products/services
    • Today – info also flows upstream and downstream, in real-time
    • Reduces uncertainty, improving efficiency
  3. Cash Flow:
    • Management of working capital
    • Traditionally one-directional, backwards up the supply chain
    • Better information flow has led to supply chain compression
    • Faster order cycle times and faster cash flow
  4. Demand Flow:
    • Detect and understand demand signals
    • Synchronize demand with supply
    • Enabled by growth and advancements in technology
    • Critical for accurate forecasting and order fulfillment

Flow Integration: Supply chain performance improves when flows are integrated:

  • Faster information improves product flow
  • Demand visibility reduces excess inventory
  • Faster cash flow improves financial performance

Consequences of Poor Flow Coordination:

  • Stockouts
  • Excess inventory
  • Delivery delays
  • Higher operating costs
  • Customer dissatisfaction

Key Takeaways

  1. Supply chains are strategic assets that can create competitive advantage for organisations of all types (private, public, nonprofit)
  2. Six major external forces drive change: globalization, technology, organization consolidation, empowered consumers, government policy, and sustainability
  3. SCM evolved from physical distribution (1960s) to integrated logistics (1980s) to full supply chain management (1990s+)
  4. The total cost concept is central to SCM decision-making - optimize the entire system, not individual activities
  5. 11 major issues must be managed for SCM to be effective, from network design to talent management
  6. Flow integration (product, information, cash, demand) is critical for supply chain performance
  7. The bullwhip effect is a major challenge that can be mitigated through improved visibility and information sharing
